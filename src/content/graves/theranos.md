## The Autopsy

Theranos is not a story of a product that failed. It is a story of a product that *never existed* — and yet convinced the world, for fifteen years, that it did.

### What Killed It

**1. The Edison Machine Was a Prop**

Holmes's proprietary blood-testing device, the Edison, simply did not work. In internal tests, results were wildly inaccurate. When Walgreens needed real results for patients, Theranos secretly used commercially available Siemens machines — diluting finger-prick blood samples to volumes the Siemens could handle, which introduced further errors. The product was theater.

**2. A Culture of Fear and Secrecy**

Employees were segmented into silos, forbidden from discussing their work, and monitored by security and lawyers. Whistleblowers like Tyler Shultz (grandson of board member George Shultz) faced legal threats and private investigators. In healthy companies, transparency breeds trust. At Theranos, paranoia was the operating system.

**3. The Narrative Trap**

Holmes's black turtleneck, her baritone voice, her Stanford dropout mythos — all carefully constructed. The media and investors *wanted* the story to be true so badly that they suspended skepticism. Theranos exploited progressive narratives (female founder, healthcare for all) as shields against scrutiny.

**4. Regulatory Evasion**

Theranos operated in a gray zone, claiming its technology was "laboratory-developed" and thus exempt from FDA approval for years. When regulators finally inspected, they found conditions so dangerous that CMS banned Holmes from the industry.

### The Afterlife

Holmes was convicted of fraud in 2022 and sentenced to 11 years in prison. The story became a bestselling book, an HBO documentary, and a Hulu series. Theranos remains the definitive cautionary tale of Silicon Valley's darkest impulses: the worship of narrative over substance, and the belief that ambition alone can bend reality.

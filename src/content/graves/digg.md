## The Autopsy

Digg was the Reddit before Reddit. In 2008, it was one of the most influential websites on the internet. 40 million monthly visitors. A front page that could crash servers. A community of power users who curated the web's best content.

Then Digg launched version 4. And in 30 days, it destroyed everything.

### What Killed It

**1. The v4 Redesign**

In August 2010, Digg launched its fourth major redesign. The changes were sweeping:
- **Removed the "bury" button**: Users could no longer downvote spam or low-quality content
- **Pushed publisher content**: Major publishers like Wired and The Washington Post got automatic front-page placement
- **Killed user submissions**: Individual users could no longer submit stories directly to the front page
- **Changed the algorithm**: The new system favored publisher feeds over community voting
- **Redesigned the UI**: The familiar ranked list was replaced with a Twitter-like feed

Digg v4 wasn't an iteration. It was a completely different product that replaced the old one overnight.

**2. The User Revolt**

The reaction was immediate and catastrophic. Power users — the people who submitted, voted, and curated Digg's content — were furious. The #diggbar hashtag trended on Twitter. Users coordinated a mass exodus to Reddit. Some users even created scripts to automatically submit every Reddit front-page story to Digg as an act of protest.

Kevin Rose, Digg's founder, later admitted: "We launched v4 too early. We didn't listen to the community."

**3. The Reddit Migration**

Reddit had been Digg's smaller, nerdier competitor for years. When Digg v4 launched, Reddit's traffic spiked. The famous Reddit post "Dear Digg, thanks for the new users" hit the front page. Many of Digg's most active power users became Reddit moderators.

Digg didn't just lose users. It gave them to its biggest competitor.

**4. The $500,000 Fire Sale**

By 2012, Digg was a shell. Staff had been cut by 37%. Traffic had collapsed. The brand was toxic. In July 2012, Betaworks acquired Digg for $500,000.

To put that in perspective: Digg had raised $45 million. Its peak valuation was $160 million. It was sold for less than the annual salary of a single senior engineer in San Francisco.

### The Afterlife

Betaworks rebuilt Digg into a respectable but forgettable news aggregator. It was sold again to Privacy Labs in 2025. But the original Digg — the community, the culture, the front page that could crash servers — died on August 25, 2010.

The lesson is carved in startup stone: **never redesign away your core users' power**. Digg didn't fail because of competition. It failed because it chose publishers over people.

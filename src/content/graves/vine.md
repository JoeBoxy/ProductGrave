## The Autopsy

Vine didn't fail because it was a bad product. It failed because it was a great product owned by the wrong company. In six seconds, Vine created an entire culture — looping comedy, memes, music discovery, and a generation of internet-famous creators. Then Twitter unplugged it.

### What Killed It

**1. The Twitter Acquisition Trap**

Twitter bought Vine in October 2012 for approximately $30 million — before Vine had even launched. The founders got rich; the product got a parent company that didn't know what to do with it. As Twitter struggled with its own user growth and monetization, Vine was perpetually under-resourced. No dedicated team. No clear strategy. Just a beloved app floating in corporate purgatory.

**2. The Instagram Ambush**

In June 2013, Instagram — already Facebook-owned and growing exponentially — added 15-second video. The timing was devastating. Instagram had a larger user base, better filters, and a more mature ecosystem. Vine's six-second constraint was its charm, but it was also its limitation. When users could post longer, prettier videos on Instagram, many simply stopped opening Vine.

**3. Creator Exodus**

Vine birthed stars: Logan Paul, Lele Pons, King Bach, Shawn Mendes. But Vine offered them zero way to make money. No ads. No creator fund. No tipping. Meanwhile, YouTube offered AdSense revenue, and Instagram offered brand sponsorships. By 2015, the top 50 Viners had mostly left for greener pastures. A social platform without its stars is just empty software.

**4. The Shutdown Announcement**

In October 2016, Twitter announced Vine's shutdown. The decision was so abrupt that even the founders learned about it on Twitter. The community was outraged. A "Save Vine" petition garnered hundreds of thousands of signatures. It didn't matter. Twitter needed to cut costs, and Vine was expendable.

**5. The Archive Erasure**

In 2019, Twitter permanently removed the Vine archive. Billions of loops, millions of hours of creativity, gone. The internet mourned. But by then, TikTok had already filled the void Vine left behind.

### The Afterlife

Vine's DNA lives everywhere. TikTok is essentially Vine with a better algorithm and Chinese funding. Instagram Reels is Vine with Facebook's ad machine. YouTube Shorts is Vine with Google's infrastructure. But none of them have Vine's raw, chaotic, democratic energy. Vine proved that constraints breed creativity — and that corporations can kill culture with a single spreadsheet decision.

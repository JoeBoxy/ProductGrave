# Webvan

An online grocery delivery pioneer that raised nearly $800 million, built state-of-the-art automated warehouses, and filed for bankruptcy just 18 months after its IPO. The ultimate cautionary tale of scaling before product-market fit.

## The Rise

**1996-06** — Founded by Louis Borders (also founder of Borders bookstore)

**1999-06** — Launches in San Francisco with 30-minute delivery promise

**1999-11** — IPO raises $375M; stock doubles on first day, valuing company at $6B

## The Fall

**2000-01** — Signs $1B contract with Bechtel to build warehouses across 26 cities

**2000-06** — Expands to 10 cities including Chicago, LA, Seattle, Atlanta

**2001-01** — Reports net loss of $217M in Q1; accumulated deficit of $830M

**2001-07** — Files for Chapter 11 bankruptcy and shuts down all operations

## Key Lessons

### Don't build infrastructure before demand

Webvan spent $1 billion on warehouses before proving the model worked in a single city. Modern grocery delivery (Instacart) uses existing stores.

### Unit economics matter more than growth

The company had a 5:1 expense-to-revenue ratio. Low-margin grocery + massive CapEx = guaranteed failure.

### Being too early is the same as being wrong

Online grocery delivery was a good idea in 1996, but consumer habits, broadband penetration, and smartphones weren't ready.

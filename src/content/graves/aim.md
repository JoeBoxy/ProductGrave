# AIM

AOL Instant Messenger, the dominant instant messaging platform of the late 1990s and 2000s. At its peak, AIM had 18 million users and defined internet culture with away messages, buddy lists, and emoticons. Killed by mobile messaging apps and AOL's decline.

## The Rise

**1997-05** — Launched as standalone app alongside AOL dial-up service

**1998-01** — Opens to non-AOL users; explosive growth begins

**2001-01** — Peak of 18 million simultaneous users

**2005-01** — Competition from MSN Messenger, Yahoo Messenger, and Google Talk intensifies

## The Fall

**2008-01** — Facebook Chat and SMS begin eating AIM's user base

**2011-01** — Microsoft buys Skype; AIM becomes increasingly irrelevant

**2017-10** — AOL announces AIM will shut down on December 15

**2017-12** — Officially shut down after 20 years

## Key Lessons

### Desktop messaging died with the desktop

AIM was a desktop app in a mobile world. WhatsApp, iMessage, and Facebook Messenger won because they were always in your pocket.

### Away messages were the original social media

AIM's away message culture — song lyrics, mood updates, inside jokes — prefigured Facebook status updates and Twitter by a decade.

### AOL couldn't save anything it owned

AOL was a declining dial-up company trying to run a messaging platform. They lacked the vision and resources to compete with mobile-first competitors.

## The Autopsy

Google+ was Google's most ambitious social play — and its most expensive lesson in the physics of network effects. With unlimited engineering talent, deep integration across Google products, and a billion-user distribution channel, it still failed. The reason wasn't technology. It was humanity.

### What Killed It

**1. The Empty Party Problem**

Google+ launched to massive initial buzz. 10 million users in two weeks. Tech journalists praised Circles. But when the average user showed up, they found a beautiful room with no one they knew inside. Their friends were on Facebook. Their family was on Facebook. Their photos, events, and memories were on Facebook. Google+ was a better house in a worse neighborhood.

**2. The Integration Backlash**

Google's strategy shifted from "attract users" to "force users." YouTube comments required Google+. Gmail contacts were ported over. Google Photos pushed sharing to Plus. Each integration created a surge in signups — and a surge in anger. Users created accounts to comment on videos, then never returned to the social network. Engagement metrics were inflated; actual usage was minimal.

**3. Circles Were Clever But Complicated**

The core innovation was "Circles" — drag-and-drop friend categorization. It was intellectually satisfying and privacy-respecting. But it required work. Facebook's single friend list was "good enough" for 99% of users. Google+ solved a problem that power users cared about and casual users didn't even notice.

**4. The Ghost Town Spiral**

By 2013, it was clear that Google+ wasn't catching up. Early adopters drifted back to Twitter and Facebook. The multi-column redesign in 2013 was widely panned. When Vic Gundotra left in 2014, the writing was on the wall. Google+ became a zombie product — technically alive, socially dead.

**5. The Final Blow**

A 2018 data breach exposing user data gave Google the cover to accelerate shutdown. But the breach didn't kill Google+; it merely provided the excuse. The product had been dying for years.

### The Afterlife

Google+ taught Silicon Valley that network effects are a moat that even unlimited money can't cross. Google's subsequent social attempts — Google Spaces, Google Allo, Google Hangouts rebrands — all carried the stench of Plus. Today, the only remnants are scattered Google Account profile pages and a cautionary tale told in product strategy meetings: "Remember Circles."

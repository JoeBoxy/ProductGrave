## The Autopsy

Google Glass was not a bad idea. In fact, it was arguably *too good* an idea — a vision of the future delivered five years before the infrastructure, social norms, and use cases were ready.

### What Killed It

**1. The Privacy Panopticon**

Glass arrived in a pre-NDA society where smartphones were already eroding privacy norms. The idea that anyone could be recorded without consent — silently, via a wink or voice command — made people viscerally uncomfortable. Bars posted "No Glass" signs. The term "Glasshole" entered the lexicon. Google had built a panopticon you wore on your face.

**2. The $1,500 Hobby**

The Explorer Edition pricing was defensible as a developer kit, but Google marketed it like a consumer product. For that price, buyers got a device with terrible battery life, a glowing eye that made users look cyborgian, and an app ecosystem that never materialized. It was an expensive toy with no toy store.

**3. No Use Case**

Google showed demos of skydiving and firefighting, but the average user's day-to-day life didn't benefit from a forehead-mounted screen. Notifications were more annoying than helpful. Photos were worse than a phone's. It solved no problem and created several new ones.

**4. Fashion vs. Function**

Google partnered with Luxottica, but the frames were still unmistakably nerdy. Wearables only work if people actually want to wear them. Glass looked like a medical device, not a fashion accessory.

### The Afterlife

Google Glass didn't truly die — it was reincarnated as an enterprise tool for factory workers and surgeons. The consumer dream was buried, but the technology lived on in ARCore, Google Lens, and eventually the broader AR industry. Sometimes a product is simply a messenger, sent to tell the world what the future might look like — and then executed for its optimism.

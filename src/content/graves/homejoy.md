## The Autopsy

Homejoy was one of the most promising startups of the early 2010s sharing economy boom. It went through Y Combinator. It raised $40 million from Google Ventures. It expanded to 30+ cities. And then it died because it fundamentally misunderstood what it was selling.

Homejoy wasn't selling clean houses. It was selling trust. And trust cannot be gig-ified.

### What Killed It

**1. The Trust Deficit**

Homejoy's model was simple: book a cleaner online, they show up at your house, they clean, you rate them. The problem? Cleaners rotated constantly. A homeowner might get a different person every single week. There was no relationship, no accountability, and no incentive for the cleaner to do exceptional work.

Contrast this with the traditional house cleaner model: the same person comes every week for years. They know your house. They know your preferences. You trust them with your keys. Homejoy threw all of that away in pursuit of "scalability."

**2. The Regulatory Guillotine**

In March 2015, the California Labor Commissioner's Office issued a ruling that a single Uber driver was an employee, not a contractor. The ruling sent shockwaves through the gig economy. For Homejoy, it was fatal.

If cleaners were employees, Homejoy would owe payroll taxes, workers' compensation, unemployment insurance, and overtime pay. Their unit economics — already shaky — became impossible. The company announced its shutdown just three months later.

**3. The Deal-Seeker Trap**

Homejoy acquired customers through deep first-time discounts — often 50% or more off the first cleaning. This attracted price-sensitive users who had no intention of becoming regular customers. They'd use the discount, give a mediocre rating, and never return. Customer acquisition cost was high; lifetime value was near zero.

**4. The Supply Side Collapse**

Cleaners hated Homejoy. The pay was low, the scheduling was erratic, and there was no path to building a client base. The best cleaners left for traditional cleaning companies or started their own businesses. The remaining cleaners were often inexperienced or unreliable. Quality dropped. Complaints rose. Homejoy's core promise — a clean house — became a gamble.

**5. Google's Acquisition**

Homejoy shut down in July 2015. Google acquired some of its technology and patents for an undisclosed sum — reportedly a small fraction of the $40M invested. The Cheung siblings moved on. Adora later revealed that lawsuits over worker classification were the primary reason for the shutdown.

### The Afterlife

Homejoy's failure had a chilling effect on the "Uber for X" boom. Investors became wary of service marketplaces. The regulatory questions it raised still haunt gig economy companies today. But the most lasting lesson is simpler: some transactions require human relationships. You can't algorithm your way out of trust.

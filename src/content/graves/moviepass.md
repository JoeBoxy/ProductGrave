## The Autopsy

MoviePass is the most spectacular pricing disaster in consumer startup history. It sold a $100+ product for $9.95, acquired 3 million customers in six months, burned through $300 million, and died.

The truly shocking part? Everyone saw it coming. The business model was obviously impossible. And they did it anyway.

### What Killed It

**1. The Math Was Never Close**

MoviePass's business model was simple: users paid $9.95/month. MoviePass paid theaters full price for every ticket, typically $10–$15. If a user saw one movie, MoviePass broke even. If they saw two, MoviePass lost $10–$20.

The average MoviePass subscriber saw 2.5 movies per month. That meant MoviePass lost approximately $15 per user per month. With 3 million subscribers, they were losing $45 million per month.

The plan? "We'll get so big that theaters will share revenue with us."

Theaters laughed. They had no incentive to share revenue. MoviePass was bringing in customers who were already going to theaters — just now at a massive discount funded by venture capital.

**2. The Outage**

In July 2018, MoviePass literally ran out of money. Users showed up at theaters and their MoviePass cards were declined. The app displayed error messages. Social media exploded with angry customers who had driven to theaters specifically because of MoviePass.

The company had to take out an emergency $5 million loan just to keep the lights on. The story went viral. The brand was permanently damaged.

**3. The Death Spiral**

After the outage, MoviePass tried to survive by introducing restrictions:
- Peak pricing (surge pricing for popular movies)
- Photo verification (users had to photograph their ticket stubs)
- Limited movie selection
- Restrictions on seeing the same movie twice

Each restriction saved a little money. Each restriction also drove away more users. By August 2018, MoviePass had gone from "unlimited movies" to "three movies per month, no blockbusters, and we might randomly decline your card."

**4. The AMC Revenge**

AMC Theaters, America's largest theater chain, had warned MoviePass that the model was unsustainable. When MoviePass collapsed, AMC launched AMC Stubs A-List — a subscription service priced at $19.95/month for three movies per week. It was profitable from day one because AMC owned the theaters.

MoviePass's own success proved the market existed. But only the theater chains could serve it profitably.

### The Afterlife

MoviePass became a verb: "to MoviePass" something means to sell a product at a loss to acquire users, then run out of money. Stacy Spikes, the original founder, eventually bought the MoviePass brand out of bankruptcy and attempted a relaunch in 2022. It failed again.

The lesson is simple: if your business model requires your suppliers to subsidize your customers, you don't have a business. You have a coupon company.

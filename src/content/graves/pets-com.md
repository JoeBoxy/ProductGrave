## The Autopsy

Pets.com is the patron saint of dot-com excess. It raised hundreds of millions of dollars. It hired a legendary advertising agency. It bought a Super Bowl commercial. It created one of the most memorable mascots in advertising history. And then it lost $300 million selling dog food at a loss and shut down within two years.

### What Killed It

**1. The Math Didn't Work**

Pets.com's core business was selling pet food and supplies online. The problem: pet food is heavy, bulky, and low-margin. A 50-pound bag of dog food might retail for $25. Shipping it cost $10–$15. Pets.com often sold it for $20 with free shipping. They were losing $5–$10 on every bag they sold.

The business plan assumed customers would buy high-margin items like toys and flea medication alongside the food. They didn't. The average order value was too low, shipping costs were too high, and repeat purchase rates were abysmal.

**2. The Grocery Store Problem**

The fundamental question Pets.com never adequately answered: Why would someone wait 3–5 days for dog food delivery when the grocery store is 5 minutes away? Pet owners don't impulse-buy 50-pound bags of kibble. They buy it when they need it. The convenience of online ordering was offset by the inconvenience of planning ahead.

**3. The Sock Puppet**

The Pets.com sock puppet mascot — voiced by Michael Ian Black — was genuinely brilliant marketing. It appeared in a $1.2 million Super Bowl ad, on a Macy's Thanksgiving Day Parade float, and in a Times Square billboard. It was so popular that people wanted to buy the puppet itself.

But here's the thing: the mascot was more popular than the company. People remembered the puppet. They didn't remember to buy dog food from the website. Pets.com spent $11 million on advertising in the first 9 months of 2000 alone. Their revenue was $5.8 million.

**4. Amazon's Shadow**

Amazon was both an investor and a competitor. Jeff Bezos saw pet supplies as a natural extension of Amazon's catalog. When Amazon began selling pet food directly, Pets.com couldn't compete on price, selection, or shipping. Amazon could lose money on pet food forever. Pets.com had about 18 months of runway.

**5. The Bubble Bursts**

Pets.com went public on February 11, 2000. The NASDAQ peaked on March 10, 2000. By November, Pets.com's stock had fallen from $14 to under $1. The IPO had raised $82.5 million, but it was the last money they ever saw. The capital markets had frozen.

### The Afterlife

The Pets.com sock puppet lives on in internet history as the symbol of dot-com delusion. The domain was sold for $35,000. The technology was worthless. But the lesson endures: you cannot advertise your way out of bad unit economics. Not with $300 million. Not with a puppet.

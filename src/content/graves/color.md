## The Autopsy

Color is the most expensive startup failure you've never heard of. It raised $41 million from Sequoia Capital — before the app even launched. Then it launched. And nobody understood what it was for.

### What Killed It

**1. The $41M Mystery**

In March 2011, TechCrunch reported that a new startup called Color had raised $41 million in a pre-launch Series A. The amount was staggering. Instagram, at the time, had raised $7 million. Path had raised $8.5 million. Color had raised five times as much — for an app that didn't exist yet.

The funding was driven entirely by founder Bill Nguyen's reputation. He had sold Lala, a music streaming service, to Apple for $80 million. Sequoia Capital assumed lightning would strike twice. They didn't ask what Color actually did.

**2. The Unintelligible Product**

Color launched on March 24, 2011. The app was a photo-sharing tool with a twist: instead of following friends, you saw photos from strangers who were physically near you. The idea was that if you were at a concert, you'd see photos from everyone else at the concert.

The reality? A confusing interface, no friends, no context, and photos from people you didn't know and didn't care about. The App Store reviews were immediate and brutal:
- "I don't understand what this does."
- "Why am I looking at photos from random people?"
- "Deleted after 5 minutes."

Color's own marketing couldn't explain the product. The website said: "Color is a miraculous, free application..." It didn't say what it was for.

**3. The Proximity Fallacy**

Color's core assumption was that physical proximity = social connection. This was catastrophically wrong. Being in the same room as someone doesn't mean you want to see their photos. In fact, it's often the opposite — you want to see photos from friends who *aren't* there.

Instagram solved this by using the follow graph. Facebook solved it with the friend graph. Color ignored both and invented a graph based on GPS coordinates. Nobody asked for it.

**4. The Pivot Spiral**

After the launch disaster, Color pivoted. Then pivoted again. Then again:
- **April 2011**: "Color is a Facebook photo app!"
- **September 2011**: "Color is a group video app!"
- **September 2012**: "Color is a live video broadcasting tool for Facebook!"

Each pivot was more desperate than the last. The user base remained near zero. The $41 million burned through engineering salaries, office space, and failed experiments.

**5. The Apple Fire Sale**

In October 2012, Apple acquired Color's engineering team for approximately $7 million — not for the app, not for the patents, but for about 20 engineers. The app was shut down. The investors lost nearly everything. Bill Nguyen had already moved on.

### The Afterlife

Color became a verb in Silicon Valley: "to Color" something means to raise massive funding for a product nobody wants. It is the ultimate example of what happens when investor FOMO replaces user validation. The $41 million didn't build a product. It built a tombstone.

# Turing Phone

A smartphone startup that promised a device made from 'Liquidmorphium' alloy with military-grade security. Despite multiple pre-order campaigns and bold claims, only a handful of units ever shipped and the company collapsed amid accusations of fraud.

## The Rise

**2014-01** — Turing Robotic Industries founded by Steve Chao

**2015-04** — Turing Phone announced with 'Liquidmorphium' body and end-to-end encryption

**2015-07** — Pre-orders open at $610; shipping promised by December

## The Fall

**2015-12** — Shipping delayed; switches from Android to Sailfish OS

**2016-08** — Announces Turing Phone Cadenza with impossible specs (two Snapdragon 830s, 12GB RAM)

**2017-01** — Claims to have developed 'Turing Monolith Chaconne' with 18GB RAM

**2018-01** — Company effectively dissolved; few if any phones delivered

## Key Lessons

### 'Liquid metal' is marketing, not engineering

Turing's 'Liquidmorphium' was essentially standard aluminum alloy with a fancy name. The material claims were pseudoscience.

### Escalating impossible claims are a red flag

When a company announces progressively more absurd products without shipping the first one, it's a sign of desperation or fraud.

### Security claims need third-party verification

Turing claimed 'unhackable' encryption. No independent security researcher ever verified this. In security, unverified claims are worthless.

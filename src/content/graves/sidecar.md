## The Autopsy

Sidecar is the most unfair death in startup history. It invented modern ridesharing. Uber copied it. Lyft copied it. Then both companies raised billions of dollars and buried the original innovator.

### What Killed It

**1. The Invention That Didn't Matter**

Sidecar launched in 2012 with ideas that seem obvious today but were revolutionary then:
- Ordinary people driving their own cars for money
- GPS tracking of your incoming ride
- Upfront pricing estimates
- Driver and passenger ratings
- In-app payments

Uber at the time was a black-car service for the wealthy. Sidecar invented the model that would become UberX. Uber's response was simple: copy everything and outspend the inventor by 50x.

**2. The Capital Armageddon**

Sidecar raised approximately $35 million total. Uber raised over $15 billion. Lyft raised over $4 billion. In a market where customer acquisition costs are astronomical and subsidies are the weapon of choice, Sidecar was bringing a spreadsheet to a nuclear war.

Uber could afford to lose $2 billion a year in China alone. Sidecar couldn't afford to lose $2 million a month in San Francisco.

**3. The Regulatory Squeeze**

Sidecar played by the rules. It worked with regulators, sought permits, and tried to build a sustainable business. Uber broke the rules, flooded cities with drivers, and let lawyers sort it out later. This "ask forgiveness, not permission" strategy required legal budgets that Sidecar simply didn't have.

**4. The Network Effect Trap**

Ridesharing is a classic two-sided network effect market. Riders want the shortest wait time; drivers want the most ride requests. Once Uber and Lyft reached critical mass in a city, Sidecar's wait times were longer, which meant fewer riders, which meant fewer drivers, which meant longer wait times. The death spiral was mathematical.

**5. The Delivery Pivot**

In early 2015, Sidecar admitted ridesharing defeat and pivoted to deliveries. But delivery was already crowded with better-funded competitors. Sidecar lacked the restaurant relationships of DoorDash, the scale of UberEats, and the focus of Postmates. The pivot was a Hail Mary that missed by miles.

### The Afterlife

General Motors acquired Sidecar's assets for a reported $39 million — barely more than Sidecar's total funding. The engineering team went on to build GM's Maven car-sharing service. Sunil Paul, Sidecar's founder, became a vocal critic of Uber's business practices.

But the cruelest irony? Sidecar's innovations are now used billions of times a day — by Uber and Lyft. The pioneer died so the copycats could thrive.

## The Autopsy

Rdio was the music streaming service that music lovers preferred — and that the masses never discovered. With a stunning interface, thoughtful social features, and a catalog that rivaled Spotify, Rdio should have won. But it was built by music lovers, not by growth hackers. And in streaming, growth wins.

### What Killed It

**1. The Freemium Onslaught**

Rdio launched with a simple model: pay $5–$10 per month, get unlimited music. No ads. No free tier. It was clean, fair, and sustainable.

Then Spotify came to America in 2012 with a freemium model: free music with ads, or premium without. Users flooded to Spotify. Rdio's team believed that a paid-only model would attract "serious" music fans. They were wrong. The serious fans went to Spotify too — because that's where everyone else was.

By 2015, Spotify had 75 million users (20 million paid). Rdio had approximately 6 million. The gap was insurmountable.

**2. Apple's Entrance**

In June 2015, Apple Music launched with three massive advantages:
- **Pre-installed** on every iPhone
- **3-month free trial** with automatic billing
- **Deep iOS integration** with Siri and the Music app

Apple didn't need to win users over. It just needed to not lose them. Within months, Apple Music had 10 million subscribers. Rdio, already struggling, lost its last chance to build a viable user base.

**3. The Economics of Scale**

Music streaming is a brutal business. Licensing costs are roughly 70% of revenue. That means you need enormous scale to cover operating costs. Spotify could negotiate better rates because of its size. Rdio, smaller and weaker, paid more per stream and earned less per user.

Rdio raised $125 million total. Spotify raised over $2 billion. The music industry is a capital war, and Rdio brought a knife.

**4. The Social Feature Paradox**

Rdio's social features were genuinely ahead of their time. You could see what your friends were listening to, follow their playlists, and discover music through their activity. It was like Spotify's "Friend Activity" panel — but Rdio had it first.

The problem? Social features require a critical mass of active users. Rdio's network was too small. You'd follow three friends, two of whom hadn't logged in for months. The social graph was a ghost town.

**5. The Pandora Sale**

In November 2015, Rdio filed for bankruptcy and sold its assets to Pandora for $75 million — a fraction of the $125M invested. Pandora got Rdio's technology and some employees. Rdio's users got a goodbye email. The founders, Friis and Zennström, had already made billions from Skype. Rdio was an expensive hobby that ran out of money.

### The Afterlife

Rdio's design influence lives on in Apple Music's interface and Spotify's social features. Some Rdio employees joined Apple and helped shape Apple Music. But Rdio itself is gone — a beautiful product in a market where beauty is the first thing to die.

## The Autopsy

Yik Yak was Secret for college campuses — and it failed for the exact same reasons, just faster and louder. Founded by two fraternity brothers at Furman University, it became the most popular app on American college campuses in 2014. Then it became the most banned app on American college campuses in 2015.

### What Killed It

**1. The Campus Panopticon**

Yik Yak's hook was proximity: you saw anonymous posts from people within a 5-mile radius. On a college campus, that meant everyone in your dorms, your classes, and your dining halls.

The result was predictable. Students posted:
- Rumors about classmates' sexual activity
- Racist and sexist slurs
- Specific threats against individuals
- Bomb threats and shooting threats

Because the radius was small, everyone knew the poster was "someone here" — which made the rumors feel more credible and the threats feel more real. Yik Yak didn't just enable bullying. It weaponized campus paranoia.

**2. The Bomb Threats**

In March 2015, a high school student in Colorado was arrested after posting a threat of a mass shooting on Yik Yak. Similar incidents followed at colleges across the country. The FBI got involved. Schools began treating Yik Yak as a security threat, not a social app.

The founders' response? Geofencing. They added technology to block the app within school boundaries. Students walked 50 feet off campus and posted anyway. The problem wasn't location. It was anonymity.

**3. The $400M Valuation Trap**

In November 2014, Yik Yak raised $62 million at a $400 million valuation from Sequoia Capital. Sequoia had missed Snapchat. They weren't going to miss the next campus sensation.

But the valuation created impossible expectations. Yik Yak needed to grow beyond campuses to justify $400M. It couldn't. Adults didn't want anonymous neighborhood gossip. The app was only relevant in dense college environments.

**4. The Square Fire Sale**

By late 2016, Yik Yak was dying. Layoffs. Plummeting usage. Advertisers fleeing. In April 2017, Square acquired Yik Yak's engineering team for approximately $1 million — reportedly less than the value of the office furniture. The app was shut down immediately.

Two years after a $400M valuation, Yik Yak was sold for scrap.

### The Afterlife

Yik Yak was briefly relaunched in 2021 with a new owner and mandatory usernames — essentially removing the anonymity that made it popular. It shut down again in 2024. The anonymous social era is over, killed by the same lesson Yik Yak and Secret learned: when you remove accountability from human communication, you don't get honesty. You get cruelty.

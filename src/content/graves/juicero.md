## The Autopsy

Juicero is the perfect parable of Silicon Valley hubris. It took something simple — squeezing a bag of chopped fruits and vegetables — and added Wi-Fi, a QR code scanner, a subscription model, and $120 million in venture capital. Then a Bloomberg reporter squeezed the bag with her bare hands and the entire company collapsed.

### What Killed It

**1. The Hand-Squeeze Revelation**

On April 19, 2017, Bloomberg published a 45-second video. Two hands. One Juicero pack. A gentle squeeze. Juice poured out. The video went viral not because it was shocking, but because it was *obvious*.

Juicero's defense was that the machine applied 4 tons of force and scanned QR codes to ensure freshness. Users didn't care. The value proposition was juice. If hands worked just as well, the machine was a $400 paperweight.

**2. The Subscription Trap**

Juicero packs cost $5–$8 and had a shelf life of about a week. The machine refused to squeeze expired packs (via QR code validation). So users paid $400 for the privilege of being locked into an expensive, time-limited subscription. It combined the worst of hardware and SaaS business models.

**3. Founder Delusion**

Doug Evans compared himself to Steve Jobs and claimed Juicero would "change the world." He flew to organic farms to inspect produce. He reportedly refused to let investors see the machine work without a non-disclosure agreement. The founder's reality distortion field was so strong that $120M flowed in without anyone asking a barista to try the product.

**4. Solving a Problem Nobody Had**

Cold-pressed juice was already available at grocery stores, juice bars, and via cheaper home juicers. Juicero didn't improve taste, nutrition, or convenience. It added Wi-Fi to a bag. The problem it solved was "how do we spend $120M?"

### The Afterlife

Juicero became the mascot of Silicon Valley excess. "Juicero for X" entered the startup lexicon as shorthand for over-engineered, VC-backed nonsense. Doug Evans moved on to other wellness ventures. The juice packs, ironically, outlived the company — former employees reported squeezing them by hand for months after the shutdown.

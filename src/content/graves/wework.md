## The Autopsy

WeWork was the most spectacular startup implosion of the 2010s. Not because it was a fraud like Theranos, or a bubble like Pets.com — but because it was, at its core, a perfectly ordinary real estate subleasing business that convinced the world it was a technology company worth $47 billion.

### What Killed It

**1. The 'Tech Company' Costume**

WeWork never built technology. It built offices. It leased buildings from landlords, renovated them with hipster aesthetics, and subleased desks to freelancers and startups. The 'community' software was an afterthought. The app was a room-booking tool. There were no network effects, no proprietary algorithms, no software margins. But calling itself a "tech company" let WeWork claim tech-company valuations — 10x what a real estate company would command.

**2. The S-1 Autopsy**

In August 2019, WeWork filed its S-1 prospectus. The document was extraordinary:

- **$1.6 billion loss** in 2018 alone
- **"Community-adjusted EBITDA"** — a made-up metric that excluded marketing, admin, and design costs
- **Self-dealing**: Neumann owned buildings that WeWork leased. He trademarked "We" and sold it to his own company for $5.9 million. He borrowed hundreds of millions against his stock.
- **Governance absurdities**: Neumann's wife Rebekah would choose his successor if he died. Neumann had **20 votes per share**. The company's mission was "to elevate the world's consciousness."

The S-1 didn't just reveal financial problems. It revealed a company being run like a cult compound.

**3. SoftBank's $10B Blind Spot**

Masayoshi Son's Vision Fund poured over $10 billion into WeWork, chasing the same growth-at-all-costs strategy that worked for Uber and Alibaba. But WeWork wasn't a marketplace or a platform. It was a landlord with a Burning Man aesthetic. Son reportedly told Neumann to be "crazier" in pursuing growth. He got what he asked for.

**4. The Neumann Exit**

When the IPO collapsed and the board revolted, Neumann didn't go down with the ship. He extracted a $1.7 billion exit package — while 2,400 employees were laid off and thousands of shareholders watched their equity evaporate. It was perhaps the most lucrative failure in business history.

### The Afterlife

WeWork filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy in November 2023, 13 years after founding. The name became a verb — "to WeWork" something means to apply tech branding and venture capital to an ordinary business until it explodes. Adam Neumann moved on to a new startup called Flow, which raised $350 million from a16z. Some people, it seems, are too big to fail.

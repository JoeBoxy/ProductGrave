## The Autopsy

Quibi is the most expensive startup failure of the 2020s — and perhaps the most instructive. It proves that in tech, pedigree, capital, and star power are no substitute for asking a simple question: *Does anyone actually want this?*

### What Killed It

**1. A Solution in Search of a Problem**

Quibi's core premise was "premium video content for your commute." The problem? Commuters already had podcasts, TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, and Netflix downloads. There was no gap in the market. Quibi invented a problem — "I want 10-minute Spielberg-quality shows on my phone" — that did not exist at scale.

**2. The Pandemic Paradox**

Quibi launched on April 6, 2020. The United States had just entered nationwide lockdown. Commutes vanished. The entire value proposition — short-form mobile video for people on the go — evaporated overnight. Competitors like Disney+ and HBO Max, which offered long-form home viewing, thrived. Quibi was designed for a world that no longer existed.

**3. Arrogant Product Decisions**

Quibi refused to let users screenshot, share clips, or cast to TVs. It was locked to mobile, locked to portrait mode for many shows, and locked behind a paywall in a world of infinite free content. Every product decision screamed "we know better than you" — and users responded by deleting the app.

**4. The Content Wasn't the Hook**

Despite A-list talent (Chrissy Teigen, Liam Hemsworth, Steven Spielberg), the shows were forgettable. "Turnstyle" — Quibi's gimmick that let you switch between portrait and landscape — was a technical marvel nobody asked for. In the attention economy, gimmicks lose to authentic engagement every time.

### The Afterlife

Quibi's content library was eventually sold to Roku for a reported $100 million — roughly 6% of what was invested. The name "Quibi" became shorthand for hubristic startup failure. In death, it achieved the cultural relevance it never had in life.

# ofo

The world's first and largest dockless bike-sharing company that raised $2.2 billion and deployed millions of bikes across China and internationally. Collapsed under debt, regulatory crackdowns, and unsustainable expansion.

## The Rise

**2014-01** — Founded by Peking University students in Beijing

**2016-06** — Secures $130M Series C; begins aggressive expansion

**2017-01** — Expands to 20 Chinese cities and begins international rollout

**2017-07** — Raises $700M led by Alibaba; valuation hits $3B

## The Fall

**2018-01** — Operates in 250 cities with 10 million bikes

**2018-06** — Chinese cities begin impounding bikes due to clutter and safety issues

**2018-11** — Dai Wei placed on government credit blacklist; unable to pay suppliers

**2018-12** — Millions of users unable to retrieve deposits; company effectively bankrupt

## Key Lessons

### Asset-heavy models scale losses with scale

Every bike ofo deployed cost money to buy, maintain, and replace. More bikes meant more losses, not more profits.

### Government regulation can kill overnight

Chinese cities impounded millions of bikes and banned new deployments. ofo's entire business model depended on public space it didn't control.

### User deposits are not revenue

ofo used $1 billion in user deposits to fund operations. When users demanded refunds, the company had no cash. It was a Ponzi scheme with bicycles.

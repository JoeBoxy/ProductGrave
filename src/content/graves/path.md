## The Autopsy

Path is the tragic story of a product that was too beautiful to live. Designed by some of the best minds in Silicon Valley, praised by every design blog, and loved by early adopters — it still couldn't find enough users to survive.

In the end, Path proved that in social networking, network effects crush aesthetics every time.

### What Killed It

**1. The Design Trap**

Path was designed by Dave Morin, a former Facebook executive who understood social products intimately. The app's animations were fluid. The interface was minimal. The color palette was warm. Every interaction felt handcrafted.

Designers worshipped Path. It won awards. It was featured in Apple keynotes. But here's the problem: the average user doesn't choose a social network based on animation curves. They choose it based on where their friends are. Path was the empty restaurant with white tablecloths and no customers.

**2. The Address Book Scandal**

In February 2012, a developer discovered that Path was silently uploading users' entire address books to its servers — names, emails, phone numbers — without permission. The revelation caused an immediate firestorm.

Path apologized and deleted the data, but the damage was done. Apple later changed iOS to require explicit permission for contact access, a policy change directly triggered by Path's behavior. For a product whose entire brand was built on intimacy and trust, secretly stealing contacts was a mortal wound.

**3. The 150-Friend Limit**

Path's core concept was Dunbar's number — the theory that humans can maintain about 150 meaningful relationships. Path limited users to 150 friends (later raised to 500). It was meant to create a more intimate, meaningful network.

The problem? Social networks are not intimate by default. They're expansive. Users want to follow celebrities, reconnect with old classmates, and discover new people. Path's limitation felt like a constraint, not a feature. It was designed for the social network users *should* want, not the one they actually wanted.

**4. The Pivot to Nowhere**

By 2014, it was clear that Path wasn't growing. The company pivoted to private messaging. Then to sticker sales. Then to a photo-sharing tool. Each pivot diluted the original vision and confused the remaining users. When Kakao acquired Path in 2016, it was already a zombie product.

**5. The Korean Acquisition**

Kakao, the South Korean tech giant, bought Path for an undisclosed sum. Most observers assumed it was a small acquihire or patent purchase. Kakao never invested in growing Path. In October 2018, they simply shut it down. The most beautiful social network in history disappeared without a eulogy.

### The Afterlife

Path's design DNA lives on in countless apps. Its "flyout" menu became a standard pattern. Its animation principles influenced iOS design. Dave Morin went on to found a wellness startup. But Path itself is gone — a reminder that in consumer tech, being beautiful is not enough. You have to be unavoidable.
